The Story

The samosa didn't start in India.
It walked here.

It travelled from Central Asia, in the 13th century, in the saddlebags of Persian caravans. Then it stopped. It never left.

13th century
Central Asia

A pastry called sambusa travels with Persian caravans across the Silk Road.

14th century
Delhi Sultanate

Alauddin Khilji's chefs plate it for royalty. Ibn Battuta writes home about it.

17–19th century
Ganga plains

UP halwais claim it. Add potato. Add masala. Make it street. It becomes breakfast.

Post-1947
Allahabad, Kanpur, Lucknow

Partition brings Punjabi chole into UP's gullies. Someone breaks a samosa open. Pours chole over it. The chole samosa is born.

2026
Mumbai

Mishra Ji arrives with a kadhai, a family recipe, and one non-negotiable rule: fresh-fried, or nothing.

The dish itself

The chole samosa is younger than you think.

The samosa is 700 years old. But the chole samosa — the one you know, with warm chickpea gravy poured over a cracked-open samosa, drizzled with green chutney and sweet imli — that dish is barely 75.

Here's how it happened. Partition, 1947. Millions of Punjabi refugees moved east into UP — into Kanpur, Lucknow, Allahabad. They brought chole with them. Samosa was already there.

No one knows who did it first. Some halwai in some gully, one morning, took a metal spoon, cracked open a fresh samosa, and ladled hot chole over it. Sprinkled sev. Drizzled chutney. Sold it for two rupees.

By the 1960s, every gully from Prayagraj to Varanasi had a stall selling it. By the 1980s, it was breakfast, snack, tea-time, midnight hunger — all at once.

"चोले-समोसे के लिए कोई मौसम नहीं होता।"
"There's no season for chole samosa."

Today it's 4 AM breakfast for a rickshaw driver. 10 AM office chai partner. 4 PM student hangout. 9 PM college gate. A single dish that stretches across every hour of the day.

That's the dish we're bringing to Mumbai. Not fusion. Not modern. Not a version. The original one.

Meet Mishra Ji

He's not real.
But every Mishra Ji from Prayagraj to Kanpur is.

He wakes at 4 AM. His shop is a metal cart, or a corner storefront, or a doorway that opens into a smoke-blackened kitchen. His kadhai is brass — always brass — because iron changes the taste.

By 5 AM, the chole is already simmering. Onions caramelised the night before. Tomatoes crushed by hand. The masala is a family blend — clove, cinnamon, black cardamom, kasuri methi, a pinch of amchur — and no two halwais make it exactly the same.

By 6, the first samosa hits hot oil. The gully wakes up to the smell. College students on bicycles stop by. Rickshaw pullers pass through. Grandmothers send children with steel tiffins.

You've eaten his food. You just didn't know his name.

"I grew up in Prayagraj — a city we still call Allahabad in our heads. My mornings had a rhythm. School bus at 7:15. Chole samosa from Netram or Loknath by 7:30. Half a samosa in one hand, half a book in the other.

When I moved to Mumbai, I searched for that dish. Found decent samosa. Never found the chole. Never found the ritual. Mishra Ji Ke Samose is my way of getting my morning back."

— Shubham Mishra, Founder
Born in Prayagraj (Allahabad), UP · Now in Mumbai

Why is this called "Mishra Ji" if you sell dahi jalebi and gulab jamun too?

Because in UP, "Mishra Ji" is the family halwai — and the family halwai sells everything. Samosa. Kachori. Jalebi. Jamun. All from one shop, all from one hand. That's the tradition we're carrying.
