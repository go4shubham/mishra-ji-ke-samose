The Story

The samosa didn't start in India.
It walked here, and stayed.

It came in the saddlebags of Persian caravans in the 13th century. Somewhere between Kabul and Kanpur, it stopped travelling. North India kept it, fed it, made it its own.

13th century
Central Asia

A stuffed pastry called sambusa travels the Silk Road on horseback. Poet Amir Khusrau writes about it before it crosses the Indus.

14th century
Delhi Sultanate

Ibn Battuta tastes one in 1340 at the Sultan's court. He writes home about it.

17–19th century
The Ganga plains

Halwais on the Ganga plains take it and pack potato inside, layer in kalonji and hing, fry it in mustard oil. By dawn, every gully smells the same. Hot ghee, warm masala, morning chai.

Post-1947
UP welcomes Punjab

Partition brings millions of Punjabi refugees east. Chole arrives with them. One winter morning, a halwai cracks a samosa open and pours hot chole over it. The chole samosa is born.

2026
Mumbai

Mishra Ji lands in Mumbai. Same brass kadhai. Same masala his family has ground for three generations. Same one rule. Samosa hits the oil only when you order.

By the time chole met samosa in an Allahabad gully, the samosa had already lived seven centuries.
We're just carrying the story forward.

The dish itself

The chole samosa is younger than you think.

The samosa is seven centuries old. But the chole samosa, the one you know, with warm chickpea gravy ladled over a cracked-open samosa, streaked with green chutney and dark tamarind, that dish is barely seventy-five.

Partition, 1947. Millions of Punjabi families moved east into Kanpur, Lucknow, Prayagraj. They brought chole. Soft chickpeas in tomato, ginger, black cardamom, kasuri methi. The samosa was already there, sold hot from brass trays outside every school.

No one knows who did it first. Some halwai in some gully, one winter morning, cracked open a fresh samosa with a metal spoon and poured hot chole over it. Green chutney went on. A pinch of red imli. Sev, raw onion. Two rupees. It changed everything.

"चोले-समोसे के लिए कोई मौसम नहीं होता।"
"There's no season for chole samosa. It's every hour of every day."

Today it's the 4 AM breakfast for a rickshaw driver in Lucknow. The 10 AM chai partner for an office clerk in Kanpur. The 4 PM student hangout at Allahabad University's gate. One bowl. Every North Indian city. Every hour of the clock.

That's the bowl we're carrying to Mumbai. Not a fusion. Not a modern take. The original one.

Meet Mishraji

The face behind the brand.
Real. Not invented.

This is my father. Born in Prayagraj. Cooking chole samosa in his own kitchen for as long as I can remember. His recipe, his masala, his hands. Everything you'll taste in a Mishra Ji Ke Samose bowl came from this man's kitchen.

He wakes early. Grinds the masala fresh. Won't allow anyone to touch the tomato before he's crushed it by hand. Says the chole isn't ready until you can smell the kasuri methi from the next room.

You're going to eat his food in Mumbai. Now you know his name.

"I grew up in Prayagraj, watching him cook. My mornings had a rhythm. School bus at 7:15. His chole samosa on the plate by 7:30. Half a samosa in one hand, half a book in the other.

When I moved to Mumbai, I searched for that dish. Found decent samosa. Never found his chole. Never found the ritual. Mishra Ji Ke Samose is my way of bringing his kitchen here."

Shubham Mishra, Founder
Prayagraj, UP · Now in Mumbai
