The Story

The samosa didn't start in India.
It walked here — and stayed.

It came in the saddlebags of Persian caravans in the 13th century. Somewhere between Kabul and Kanpur, it stopped travelling. North India kept it, fed it, made it its own. Seven hundred years later, it's what wakes us up.

13th century
Central Asia

A stuffed pastry called sambusa — meat, onion, spice — travels the Silk Road on horseback. Poet Amir Khusrau writes about it before it even crosses the Indus.

14th century
Delhi Sultanate

By Muhammad bin Tughlaq's court, the sambusa is court food — served to nobility. Ibn Battuta, the Moroccan traveller, tastes one in 1340 and writes home about it.

17–19th century
The Ganga plains

The halwais of the Ganga plains make it theirs. They pack potato inside — the new crop, freshly arrived from the Portuguese. They layer in kalonji, hing, garam masala. They fry it in mustard oil until it crackles. By dawn, every gully from Prayagraj to Kanpur smells of hot ghee and morning chai.

Post-1947
UP welcomes Punjab

Partition brings millions of Punjabi refugees east — into Kanpur, Lucknow, Prayagraj. Chole comes with them. The samosa is already there. Someone, one morning, cracks a samosa open with a metal spoon and pours hot chole over it. Adds green chutney. Sprinkles sev. Charges two rupees. The chole samosa is born.

2026
Mumbai

Mishra Ji lands in Mumbai. Same brass kadhai. Same masala his family has ground for three generations. One rule he won't break: samosa hits the oil only when you order it.

By the time chole met samosa in an Allahabad gully, the samosa had already lived seven centuries.
We're just carrying the story forward.

The dish itself

The chole samosa is younger than you think.

The samosa itself is old — seven centuries old, brought by Persian caravans, cooked in Sultanate kitchens, adopted by every North Indian household. But the chole samosa — the one you know, with warm chickpea gravy ladled over a cracked-open samosa, streaked with green chutney and dark tamarind — that dish is barely seventy-five.

Here's what happened. Partition, 1947. Millions of Punjabi families moved east — into Kanpur, Lucknow, Prayagraj, Varanasi. They brought chole with them: soft chickpeas simmered in tomato, ginger, black cardamom, kasuri methi. The samosa was already waiting there, sold hot from brass trays outside every school and courthouse.

No one knows who did it first. Some halwai in some gully, one winter morning, took a metal spoon, cracked open a fresh samosa, and poured hot chole over it. Green chutney went on. A pinch of red imli followed. He sprinkled sev, threw in raw onion. He sold it for two rupees. It changed everything.

By the 1960s, every gully from Prayagraj to Varanasi had a stall making it. By the 1980s, it was breakfast, snack, tea-time, and midnight hunger — all in one bowl. It moved from Uttar Pradesh into Bihar, into Delhi, into Punjab. It became the North Indian morning. Not just a dish — a ritual.

"चोले-समोसे के लिए कोई मौसम नहीं होता।"
"There's no season for chole samosa. It's every hour of every day."

Today it's the 4 AM breakfast for a rickshaw driver in Lucknow. The 10 AM chai partner for an office clerk in Kanpur. The 4 PM student hangout at Allahabad University's gate. The 9 PM comfort food after a long shift in Delhi. One bowl. Every North Indian city. Every hour of the clock.

That's the bowl we're carrying to Mumbai. Not a fusion. Not a modern take. Not a version. The original one, made the way the halwais of the Ganga plains have made it for three generations.

Meet Mishra Ji

He's not real.
But every Mishra Ji from Prayagraj to Kanpur is.

In a gully in Prayagraj, morning smells like Mishra Ji's kitchen. Ghee crackling on a heavy brass kadhai. Ginger and green chilli crushed between his palms. The dry rasp of cumin hitting hot oil. Black cardamom cracking open like a firework. His whole neighbourhood wakes up because Mishra Ji has started frying.

By 5 AM the chole is already simmering — the onions caramelised the night before, tomatoes crushed by hand, a masala his grandfather perfected: clove, cinnamon, black cardamom, kasuri methi, a pinch of amchur for the sour finish. He tempers a fresh spoonful of ghee and cumin over every bowl. That's the smell that hits you before you sit down.

By six the first samosa goes in. The oil roars. In ninety seconds it's golden, blistered, still crackling as he lifts it out. College students on bicycles brake for it. Rickshaw pullers detour for it. Grandmothers send children with steel tiffins wrapped in a hand towel. He works for the next four hours without looking up.

You've eaten his food a hundred times. You just never knew his name.

"I grew up in Prayagraj — a city we still call Allahabad in our heads. My mornings had a rhythm. School bus at 7:15. Chole samosa from Netram or Loknath by 7:30. Half a samosa in one hand, half a book in the other.

When I moved to Mumbai, I searched for that dish. Found decent samosa. Never found the chole. Never found the ritual. Mishra Ji Ke Samose is my way of getting my morning back."

— Shubham Mishra, Founder
Born in Prayagraj (Allahabad), UP · Now in Mumbai

In the bowl

Two samosas.
Cracked open.

01

The samosa hits the oil when you order. Not two hours ago. Not warming under a lamp because we saw you walking in. Ninety seconds in 180°C ghee — that's how long it takes to become golden, blistered, crackling. Your bowl reaches you while it's still too hot to hold with bare fingers.

02

The chole has been simmering since 5 AM. Kabuli chana, softened overnight. Tomatoes crushed by hand. A masala our Prayagraj halwai blends every Monday. A fresh tempering of ghee, cumin, and dried red chilli hits your bowl the moment it's plated — you'll hear it hiss.

03

Then everything comes at once. The samosa cracks open under the spoon. Warm chole floods the shell. A ribbon of green coriander chutney. A ribbon of sweet-sour imli. Crackling sev. Ruby pomegranate. A cool spoon of dahi. You'll taste four things in one bite — hot, sweet, tangy, spicy — and none of them will let go.

Eaten with a spoon at first. Mostly with hands after. Never with a fork.

Our promise

We won't do this half-hearted.

01

Fresh-fried, or nothing.

Your samosa hits the oil the moment you order. Not two hours ago, kept warm under a lamp. If that means you wait four extra minutes — you wait. Yesterday's samosa dressed up as today's isn't a version of us.

02

Recipe locked in Prayagraj.

Our masala came from a halwai in Katra Bazaar who's been grinding it the same way for thirty years. We blind-tested it against six others. His won. Same bowl in Andheri as in Allahabad — that's the promise.

03

Whole spices, weekly grind.

Cumin, coriander, clove, black cardamom, kasuri methi — bought whole from the Khari Baoli market, ground fresh every Monday morning in our kitchen. Nothing sits longer than seven days.

04

Nothing your nani wouldn't recognise.

No preservatives. No artificial colours. No stabilisers. No "flavour enhancers" hidden in the fine print. If it wasn't in a Prayagraj kitchen in 1975, it's not in ours in 2026.

05

Same bowl in every outlet.

Kiosk 01 in Andheri. Kiosk 05 in a Powai mall. The tenth cloud kitchen in Bandra. Every bowl will taste like the first bowl our Prayagraj halwai signed off on. That consistency is the whole business.

Just so we're clear

What we're not.

  • Not a healthy option. It's fried in ghee. It's soaked in masala. It's meant to satisfy you at 8 AM and comfort you at 11 PM. Come to us honest, come to us hungry.
  • Not a fusion brand. No chocolate samosa. No pizza samosa. No sushi-inspired anything. We stay in our lane because our lane is seven hundred years old.
  • Not a discount play. Whole spices from Khari Baoli. Fresh ghee. Hand-picked pomegranates. Real coriander bought at 4 AM. Good ingredients aren't cheap, and we won't pretend otherwise by cutting corners.
  • Not the next Wow Momo. They did their food, their city, their story. We're doing ours. Different bowl entirely.
  • Not a cloud-kitchen ghost. You'll find us on the street — a real kiosk, a real signboard, a real mustache behind the counter. We want you to walk in.
